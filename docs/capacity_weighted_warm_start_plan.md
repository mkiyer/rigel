# Capacity-Weighted Warm Start — Design Plan

## Problem Recap

Phase 1 (per-fragment effective length) is implemented.  It corrects
the mathematical denominator in the E-step but has limited impact on
the pathological case (two isoforms sharing a 20 kb exon, differing
only in a 50 bp vs 300 bp first exon) because the effective lengths
are nearly identical (19,701 vs 19,951 — a 1.3% difference).

The root cause is that the EM warm start distributes ambiguous
fragments **uniformly** (1/k) across candidates.  When 997 out of
1000 T1-origin fragments land in the shared exon, each one seeds
both T1 and T2 equally.  The EM then converges near this misleading
initialization because the likelihood surface is nearly flat across
the shared region.

## Core Idea: Trapezoid Coverage Model

### Theoretical basis

Under uniform random sampling of fragments from a transcript, the
expected coverage at transcript-relative position $x$ is:

$$
c(x) = \begin{cases}
x & 0 \le x < w \\
w & w \le x < L - w \\
L - x & L - w \le x \le L
\end{cases}
$$

where $L$ is the transcript's spliced exonic length and $w = \min(f,
L/2)$ where $f$ is the fragment length.  This is the classic
**coverage trapezoid** — uniform in the middle, linearly decreasing at
the edges.

A fragment observed near a transcript edge is **less likely** to have
been generated by that transcript (low coverage capacity).  If it IS
observed anyway, it is **stronger evidence** for that transcript.

### The inverse-capacity weight

For a fragment spanning transcript-relative positions $[s, e)$, the
**mean coverage capacity** over the fragment is:

$$
\bar{c} = \frac{1}{e - s} \int_s^e c(x)\, dx
$$

The **capacity weight** is the inverse, normalized so plateau fragments
get weight 1.0:

$$
W = \frac{w}{\bar{c}}
$$

- Fragment deep in plateau: $\bar{c} = w$, so $W = 1.0$
- Fragment at 5' edge (starting at position 0): $\bar{c} < w$, so
  $W > 1.0$
- Fragment at 3' end: symmetric

### Why this resolves the pathological case

In the T1/T2 example (T1 has a 50 bp first exon, T2 has a 300 bp
first exon, both share a 20 kb second exon):

- A fragment at position [0, 200] on T1 is deep into the ramp of T1
  (only 50 bp of unique runway before the splice junction). Its
  capacity weight for T1 is high (e.g. ~3–5x), reflecting that it's
  unlikely under uniform sampling but was still observed.

- That same fragment on T2 lands in a more comfortable region (300 bp
  of runway). Its capacity weight for T2 is moderate (~1.5x).

- A fragment in the shared 20 kb exon gets weight ≈ 1.0 for both T1
  and T2.

When we sum these capacity-weighted shares across all fragments, T1
recovers its true proportion even without unique reads, because the
*geometric difficulty* of generating each fragment encodes positional
information.

## Evaluating Approaches

We considered four strategies for integrating the trapezoid weight.
The analysis below explains why we converged on the "One Virtual
Read" prior.

### Option A: Log-likelihood term — REJECTED

Adding the capacity weight to `log_lik` has intuitive appeal but
is fundamentally incorrect.

**Why position doesn't belong in the likelihood.**  The standard
generative model says: a fragment of length $l$ starts at a uniformly
random position $s \in [0, L-l]$ on transcript $T$.  The probability
of observing the fragment is:

$$P(f \mid T) \propto \frac{1}{L_T - l_f + 1} \times P(\text{alignment quality})$$

This $1/(L_T - l_f + 1)$ term is the per-fragment effective length
we implemented in Phase 1.  Under this model, **all valid start
positions are equally likely**, so knowing where the fragment falls
does not change $P(f \mid T)$.  Position is uninformative in the
likelihood.

The trapezoid describes **coverage** (how many fragments cover
position $x$), not **start probability**.  A fragment near the edge
has low coverage capacity because fewer fragments can reach that
position — but the probability of any specific fragment starting at
position $s$ is the same everywhere: $1/(L-l+1)$.

**What `log(W)` or `log(W/W_{max})` would do.**  Adding `log(W)` to
`log_lik` asserts a false model — that fragments in the middle of a
transcript are less likely to be observed.  While this empirically
pushes the posterior in the "right" direction for our pathological
case, it corrupts the generative semantics: the converged $\theta$
would no longer represent transcript proportions, but some mixture of
proportions and positional-information weighting.  This introduces
systematic bias in cases where the standard model is already correct
(the vast majority of transcripts).

**Bottom line**: The per-fragment effective length is the correct and
complete position-independent normalization.  The additional
information from the trapezoid is about *which transcript the
fragment came from* (a prior/initialization question), not about
*how likely the fragment is* (a likelihood question).

### Option B: Warm start only — SUBSUMED

Replace the uniform 1/k warm start with capacity-weighted fractional
shares.  Simple, but the EM can "forget" the initialization.  With
SQUAREM's aggressive extrapolation, $\theta$ may drift in the flat-
likelihood region.  Subsumed by Option D.

### Option C: Warm start + gamma parameter — SUBSUMED

Add a persistent capacity-weighted Dirichlet prior
$\alpha_t = \alpha_0 + \gamma \cdot C_t$.  Correct but requires
the user to tune $\gamma$, which passes the buck.  Subsumed by
Option D with a specific, principled choice of $\gamma$.

### Option D: "One Virtual Read" prior — SELECTED

**Core insight**: Option C's $\gamma$ parameter has a natural,
parameter-free value: $\gamma = 1/N_{\text{ambig}}$.

**Procedure**:

1. For each ambiguous fragment, compute capacity weights for all
   candidates, normalize to sum to 1.0 (fractional shares).
2. Sum all fractional shares across all $N$ fragments →
   $C_t$ (vector of length $n_{\text{components}}$, summing to $N$).
3. Divide by $N$ → $C_t/N$ (sums to exactly 1.0).
4. Add $C_t/N$ to the Dirichlet prior $\alpha$.

**Why this is parameter-free and bulletproof**:

- **Normalizes to exactly 1.0 total mass.**  No matter if a locus
  has 10 or 100,000 ambiguous fragments, the total prior mass added
  is exactly 1.0 "virtual read."

- **Cannot override data.**  Each real fragment contributes 1.0 to
  the M-step.  The capacity prior contributes < 1.0 total.  If
  transcript T1 has even 1 additional real read of evidence over T2,
  the data wins.

- **Breaks ties permanently.**  When the likelihood surface is flat
  (the shared-exon pathology), the EM gradient is zero.  The capacity
  prior provides a permanent, non-zero gradient:

  $$\theta_t \propto \text{unique}_t + \underbrace{\theta_t \cdot N_{\text{ambig}}}_{\text{flat E-step (tied)}} + \alpha_0 + C_t/N$$

  The $C_t/N$ asymmetry is amplified by the EM: even a tiny
  $\Delta\theta$ biases the E-step, which feeds back through the
  M-step.  The fixed point depends on the ratio
  $(C_{T1}/N + \text{unique}_{T1}) / (C_{T2}/N + \text{unique}_{T2})$.

- **Self-regulating.**  More fragments → smaller per-fragment prior
  influence → less risk of distortion.  Fewer fragments → larger
  per-fragment influence → more tie-breaking power when it's needed
  most.

- **Backward-compatible.**  When all capacity weights are 1.0 (single
  exon, all fragments in the plateau), the scaled shares reduce to
  $1/(N \cdot k)$ per candidate — a tiny uniform prior that is
  indistinguishable from the existing `em_prior`.

**Relationship to gamma**: This IS Option C with
$\gamma = 1/N_{\text{ambig}}$ automatically computed.  No user
parameter needed.

## The Trapezoid Function

### Cleaned-up implementation

```python
def compute_trapezoid_weight(
    frag_start: int,
    frag_end: int,
    transcript_length: int,
) -> float:
    """Inverse coverage-capacity weight for a fragment on a transcript.

    Uses the trapezoid coverage model: under uniform random sampling,
    the expected coverage at position x on a transcript of length L
    for fragments of length f is min(x, w, L-x) where w = min(f, L/2).

    A fragment in the plateau region gets weight 1.0.  A fragment near
    a transcript edge gets weight > 1.0, reflecting that it is less
    likely to be generated yet still observed -- stronger evidence for
    that transcript.

    Parameters
    ----------
    frag_start : int
        Fragment 5' position in transcript-relative coordinates [0, L).
    frag_end : int
        Fragment 3' position in transcript-relative coordinates (0, L].
    transcript_length : int
        Spliced exonic length of the transcript.

    Returns
    -------
    float
        Weight >= 1.0.  Plateau -> 1.0; edge -> > 1.0.
    """
    f_len = frag_end - frag_start
    if f_len <= 0 or transcript_length <= 0:
        return 1.0

    L = transcript_length
    w = min(f_len, L / 2.0)

    total_area = 0.0

    # Left ramp: c(x) = x for x in [0, w)
    s = max(0, min(frag_start, w))
    e = max(0, min(frag_end, w))
    if s < e:
        total_area += (e - s) * (s + e) / 2.0

    # Plateau: c(x) = w for x in [w, L - w)
    s = max(w, min(frag_start, L - w))
    e = max(w, min(frag_end, L - w))
    if s < e:
        total_area += (e - s) * w

    # Right ramp: c(x) = L - x for x in [L - w, L]
    s = max(L - w, min(frag_start, L))
    e = max(L - w, min(frag_end, L))
    if s < e:
        total_area += (e - s) * ((L - s) + (L - e)) / 2.0

    area_per_base = total_area / f_len
    area_per_base = max(area_per_base, 1.0)

    return w / area_per_base
```

**Key design choices**:
- `w = min(f_len, L/2)` uses the actual fragment length, not the
  mean insert.  The trapezoid shape depends on the specific fragment.
- `area_per_base = max(..., 1.0)` prevents infinity for overhang
  fragments.
- Return is `w / area_per_base` so plateau -> 1.0 exactly.

## Integration Architecture

### Capacity-weighted warm start + One Virtual Read prior

The integration has two parts, both using the same $C_t$ vector:

**Part 1: Capacity-weighted warm start** (replaces uniform 1/k):

```python
# Current (estimator.py L651-L655):
theta_init[comp_idx] += n * has_p.astype(np.float64) / k

# New:
theta_init = unique_totals.copy()
capacity_totals = np.zeros(n_total, dtype=np.float64)
n_ambiguous = 0

for comp_idx, wt_matrix, ll_matrix, _scratch in ec_data:
    n = wt_matrix.shape[0]
    n_ambiguous += n
    has_p = prior[comp_idx] > 0.0
    masked_wt = wt_matrix * has_p[np.newaxis, :]
    row_sums = masked_wt.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)
    row_sums[row_sums == 0] = 1.0
    shares = masked_wt / row_sums       # (n_units, k)
    per_comp = shares.sum(axis=0)        # (k,)
    theta_init[comp_idx] += per_comp
    capacity_totals[comp_idx] += per_comp
```

**Part 2: One Virtual Read prior** (persistent):

```python
# Add exactly 1.0 total virtual read to the prior
if n_ambiguous > 0:
    prior = prior + capacity_totals / n_ambiguous
```

No gamma.  No user parameter.  The total added mass is:
$\sum_t C_t / N = N / N = 1.0$.

### How the M-step uses both

```python
# M-step (unchanged mechanically, but prior now includes capacity term):
theta_new = unique_totals + em_totals + prior
```

The prior persists across all EM iterations, keeping the geometric
signal alive.  The warm start provides good $\theta_0$ for fast
convergence.

## Data Pipeline Requirements

### What's missing

To compute the trapezoid weight for a fragment-transcript pair, we
need the fragment's position in **transcript-relative** coordinates.
Currently this information is lost during fragment resolution:

| Data needed | Currently available? | Where |
|---|---|---|
| Fragment genomic start | **No** — discarded in `resolve_fragment()` | `Fragment.exons[0].start` exists pre-resolution |
| Fragment genomic footprint | **Yes** | `bf.genomic_footprint` |
| Transcript spliced length | **Yes** | `ctx.t_length_arr[t_idx]` |
| Transcript exon intervals | **No** — in `intervals.feather` but not per-transcript | `_iv_t_index` exists on HulkIndex |

### New data to thread through

1. **`genomic_start`** — the leftmost genomic position of the
   fragment.  Add to `ResolvedFragment`, buffer pipeline, and
   `BufferedFragment`.

2. **Per-transcript exon interval table** — a dict on `HulkIndex`
   mapping `t_index` -> sorted exon `[start, end]` pairs.  Built once
   at index load from `intervals.feather`.

3. **`genomic_to_transcript_pos()`** — utility mapping a genomic
   position to transcript-relative exonic coordinate
   (0 = 5' end, L = 3' end).

4. **`capacity_weights`** — float64 array parallel to `t_indices` /
   `log_liks` in `ScanData` and `LocusEMInput`.

### Step 2c: Implement `genomic_to_transcript_pos()`

**File**: `scoring.py`

```python
def genomic_to_transcript_pos(
    genomic_pos: int,
    exon_intervals: np.ndarray,   # (n_exons, 2) sorted [start, end]
    strand: int,
    transcript_length: int,
) -> int:
    """Map a genomic position to transcript-relative 5'->3' coordinate."""
    offset = 0
    for i in range(len(exon_intervals)):
        ex_start = int(exon_intervals[i, 0])
        ex_end = int(exon_intervals[i, 1])
        if genomic_pos < ex_start:
            break
        if genomic_pos < ex_end:
            offset += genomic_pos - ex_start
            break
        offset += ex_end - ex_start
    else:
        offset = transcript_length

    offset = max(0, min(offset, transcript_length))
    if strand == Strand.NEG:
        offset = transcript_length - offset
    return offset
```

## nRNA and gDNA Handling

- **nRNA capacity weight = 1.0**: nRNA fragments span introns and
  have no meaningful transcript-relative position in spliced
  coordinates.
- **gDNA capacity weight = 1.0**: gDNA is a single component per
  locus with no transcript-relative structure.
- **Prior-zeroed components**: The `has_p = prior[comp_idx] > 0.0`
  mask ensures components with zeroed prior (nRNA without intronic
  evidence, gDNA without antisense evidence) receive no capacity
  weight.

## Testing Strategy

### Unit tests for `compute_trapezoid_weight()`

1. Fragment centered in plateau -> weight = 1.0
2. Fragment at position [0, f_len] (leftmost) -> weight > 1.0
3. Fragment at position [L-f_len, L] (rightmost) -> same weight
   (symmetry)
4. Fragment spanning full transcript [0, L] -> expected weight
5. Short transcript (L < 2*f_len) -> w = L/2, verify correct
6. Zero-length fragment or transcript -> 1.0

### Unit tests for `genomic_to_transcript_pos()`

1. Position at first exon start -> 0 (or L for NEG strand)
2. Position in intron -> maps to preceding exon boundary
3. Position at last exon end -> L (or 0 for NEG strand)
4. Multi-exon transcript with known positions

### Scenario tests

- **Scenario A** (pathological case): T1 exons
  `[(1000,1050), (2000,22000)]`, T2 exons
  `[(1200,1500), (2000,22000)]`, abundance 10:1.
  Assert T2 relative error < 20% (currently 53%).

- **Scenario B** (no unique reads): Three overlapping isoforms where
  no fragment maps uniquely.  Assert reasonable accuracy.

- **Scenario C** (single exon control): All weights = 1.0.  Verify
  no regression.

## Risks and Mitigations

### Exon interval lookup performance

Dict lookup per candidate inside the Python scoring loop.  Unlikely
to be the bottleneck alongside existing `math.log` calls.  If
profiling shows otherwise, switch to CSR-like contiguous storage.

### Memory for exon intervals

~10 MB for human annotation (700k exons).  Acceptable.

### Capacity weights all 1.0 (no edge signal)

When all fragments land in the plateau, the One Virtual Read prior
degenerates to a tiny uniform prior indistinguishable from the
existing `em_prior`.  This is by design — the feature is a strict
improvement (helps when there's edge signal, no-op otherwise).

---

## Decisions

1. **Log-likelihood integration rejected.**  Position is
   uninformative in the standard generative model; the per-fragment
   effective length is the correct and complete normalization.  The
   trapezoid signal belongs in the prior, not the likelihood.

2. **"One Virtual Read" prior replaces the gamma parameter.**
   Total capacity prior mass is exactly 1.0, automatically scaled by
   $1/N_{\text{ambig}}$.  No user parameter to tune.

3. **Both warm start and persistent prior are used.**  The warm start
   provides good $\theta_0$ for fast convergence.  The persistent
   prior prevents the EM from drifting in flat-likelihood regions.

4. **Fragment-specific length for $w_{\max}$, not mean insert.**

5. **nRNA and gDNA capacity weights are 1.0.**

6. **`genomic_start` stored per-fragment.**  One int32 per fragment.
   Transcript-relative mapping is per-candidate at scan time.
